Executive Overview The World Energy Outlook 2025 marks a pivotal transition into the "Age of Electricity," where rising power demand, digital technologies, and electrification are reshaping the global energy landscape. The report analyzes multiple scenarios‚ÄîCurrent Policies, Stated Policies, Net Zero by 2050, and ACCESS‚Äîto assess future energy pathways, security risks, and climate outcomes.

Market & Policy Major economies, notably the United States and China, are adjusting energy policies to balance security, investment, and climate goals. The U.S. is recalibrating its approach, weighing fossil fuels and nuclear alongside renewables, while China‚Äôs policy shifts continue to influence global demand and supply chains. The report underscores that scenarios are not forecasts but tools to understand the implications of different policy and technology choices.

Energy security now encompasses not only oil and gas but also critical minerals, grid resilience, cyber threats, and weather disruptions.

Ad Space

Technology & Innovation Electricity demand is projected to outpace overall energy demand, driven by cooling needs, the electrification of transport, heating, and industry, and the rapid growth of data centers and AI. However, grid infrastructure is emerging as a bottleneck, with generation investment outpacing grid spending. This mismatch leads to congestion, delayed connections, and curtailment.

Flexibility solutions‚Äîsuch as storage, demand response, and advanced grid management‚Äîare essential, but current battery technologies cannot fully address seasonal variability.

Funding & Projects Investment trends reveal robust growth in renewable generation and digital infrastructure, but lagging grid modernization and expansion. LNG export capacity is set to surge by 2030, raising questions about long-term demand and methane leakage risks. The material footprint of the energy system remains substantial, with net zero pathways reducing extraction mainly through declining coal use.

Policy interventions are needed to diversify critical mineral supply chains, encourage recycling, and incentivize new refining capacity.

Risks & Constraints Systemic risks include high concentration in critical mineral refining, export controls, and the vulnerability of power grids to extreme weather and cyberattacks. The report highlights the persistent gap in electricity access and clean cooking, particularly in developing regions. Grid bottlenecks, material supply constraints, and diverging fossil fuel outlooks present ongoing challenges for energy security and transition.

Outlook The World Energy Outlook 2025 provides a strategic framework for navigating the complex interplay of electrification, security, and climate imperatives. Achieving sustainable transitions will require coordinated policy, investment in grids and flexibility, and proactive management of material and geopolitical risks.

The scenarios illustrate that while the pace and direction of change vary, the centrality of electricity and infrastructure resilience will define the next era of global energy. Full report: https://www.iea.
