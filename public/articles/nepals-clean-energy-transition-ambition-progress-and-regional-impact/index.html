Executive Overview Nepal is positioning itself as a regional leader in clean energy, despite being a least-developed and climate-vulnerable country. With abundant hydropower, a near-zero-carbon electricity mix, and early net-zero commitments, Nepal demonstrates how renewables can drive development, resilience, and cross-border cooperation.

Market & Policy Nepal‚Äôs third Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commits to net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2045‚Äîone of the world‚Äôs earliest targets. By 2030, Nepal aims to cut emissions substantially, conditional on international finance and technology support. Energy policy is closely aligned with climate goals, focusing on clean electricity, electrification of transport and cooking, and enhancing forest-based carbon sinks.

The government targets universal electricity access by 2030 and is shifting focus from basic access to quality and productive use, including grid upgrades and smart metering.

Technology & Innovation Hydropower dominates Nepal‚Äôs energy mix, supplying over 98% of electricity. Installed capacity reached about 3 GW in 2024, ending chronic shortages. The government aims for 15 GW of clean generation by 2030, primarily from new hydropower projects, with solar PV added to improve dry-season reliability. Solar remains a small but growing share, supported by net metering, utility-scale plants, and off-grid systems. Wind is at pilot stage.

Electrification is central to emissions reduction, with targets for 90% of new private vehicle sales and 60% of public vehicle sales to be electric by 2030. Clean cooking is also prioritized, aiming for 25% of households to use electric cooking by 2030, alongside expanded biogas use. In 2023, Nepal adopted a National Green Hydrogen Policy, launching pilots to produce hydrogen and ammonia from surplus hydropower, mainly to replace imported fertilizers.

While electrification is the immediate focus, hydrogen is viewed as a long-term industrial and export opportunity.

Funding & Projects Nepal mobilizes climate finance through international grants and concessional loans (from sources like the Green Climate Fund, World Bank, and ADB), carbon finance via REDD+ and emerging Article 6 carbon markets, and large-scale foreign investment‚Äîespecially from India. In 2025, Nepal issued its first private-sector green bond, backed by IFC. Meeting 2030 targets will require tens of billions of dollars in additional international support.

Lifeline tariffs and rural energy programs support affordability and inclusion.

Risks & Constraints Despite progress, Nepal faces significant challenges: scaling up investment, ensuring grid reliability, and managing seasonal hydropower variability. Achieving electrification targets requires infrastructure upgrades, technology transfer, and sustained policy support. The country‚Äôs goals are highly dependent on international finance and regional cooperation.

Outlook Nepal‚Äôs clean energy transition is notable for its ambition and early results. The landmark India‚ÄìNepal agreement enables up to 10 GW of electricity exports over 10 years, providing revenue and investment certainty. Nepal has also started exporting power to Bangladesh, strengthening its role as a South Asian clean energy hub. If international finance and cooperation keep pace, Nepal could become a model for renewable-driven development and regional decarbonization.
