Executive Overview The global energy sector is undergoing a rapid transformation, with renewables at the forefront. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), a surge in affordable renewable supply is poised to meet the world‚Äôs rising electricity demand and accelerate the decline of fossil fuel dominance. However, the transition is complex, requiring robust grid infrastructure, policy reforms, and targeted investments, as highlighted by recent developments in Europe and North Africa.

Market & Policy The IEA‚Äôs World Energy Outlook 2025 underscores that renewables are growing faster than any other major energy source in all scenarios, driven by falling costs, supportive policies, and climate commitments. The agency projects that renewables could satisfy nearly all incremental global electricity demand in the coming decades.

In Italy and other markets, debates continue over the reliability and integration of renewables, but policy momentum is shifting toward energy security and decarbonization. Internationally, initiatives such as the EU‚Äôs campaign to scale up renewables in Africa reflect a broader push to mobilize capital and technology for emerging markets.

Technology & Innovation The rapid expansion of solar and wind is reshaping power systems. Yet, integrating variable renewables at scale requires significant upgrades to grid infrastructure and digitalization. Grid modernization ensures reliability and enables the flexible management of intermittent resources.

In Tunisia, modernization efforts are central to the government‚Äôs energy transition strategy, with reforms targeting both operational efficiency and the adoption of advanced grid technologies.

Funding & Projects A notable example is the World Bank‚Äôs approval of the Tunisia Energy Reliability, Efficiency, and Governance Improvement Program (TEREG), a US$430 million initiative designed to accelerate renewable deployment, strengthen the national utility (STEG), and attract private investment. The program aims to mobilize US$2.8 billion in private capital, add 2.8 GW of new solar and wind capacity by 2028, and create over 30,000 jobs.

TEREG also seeks to reduce electricity supply costs, improve cost recovery, and lower state subsidies, aligning with Tunisia‚Äôs broader energy and climate goals. The project benefits from the World Bank‚Äôs Framework for Financial Incentives, recognizing its long-term emissions reduction impact. For more details, see the [official World Bank project document](https://documents.worldbank.org/en/publication/documents-reports/documentdetail/099041025150537909).

Risks & Constraints Despite strong growth, the IEA warns that electricity demand is set to rise much faster than overall energy consumption, putting pressure on existing infrastructure. Many countries are not fully prepared for this surge, risking reliability and affordability. Challenges include grid bottlenecks, financing gaps, and the need for regulatory reforms to enable private sector participation.

In some regions, public debate still questions the reliability of renewables, underscoring the importance of clear communication and robust policy frameworks.

Outlook The global shift toward renewables is accelerating, but success hinges on coordinated action across technology, policy, and finance. Countries like Tunisia demonstrate how targeted reforms and international support can unlock investment and drive sector modernization. As electricity demand grows, continued attention to grid resilience and market design will be essential to ensure a secure, sustainable energy future.

For further analysis, consult the IEA‚Äôs [World Energy Outlook 2025](https://www.iea.org/reports/world-energy-outlook-2025).
