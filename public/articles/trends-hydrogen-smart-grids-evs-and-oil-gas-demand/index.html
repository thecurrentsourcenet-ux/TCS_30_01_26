Executive Overview The global energy sector is experiencing significant shifts, with notable progress in hydrogen production, smart grid modernization, and electric vehicle (EV) infrastructure. Meanwhile, oil and gas demand projections have been revised upward, reflecting persistent reliance on fossil fuels. This report summarizes key developments and their implications for the energy transition.

Market & Policy Policy and market signals remain mixed. The International Energy Agency (IEA) has updated its forecasts, now predicting that oil and gas demand will continue to rise beyond 2030, diverging from previous expectations of an imminent peak. This change is attributed to weak climate action and ongoing energy security concerns, suggesting that without stronger policy interventions, fossil fuel consumption will remain robust for decades (see Scientific American, Financial Times).

Ad Space

In Chile, the national hydrogen association (H2 Chile) has released a policy document outlining eight strategic measures to support green hydrogen as a state policy. The proposal aims to ensure continuity and regulatory clarity for hydrogen development, highlighting the sector's potential to drive economic diversification and decarbonization. Over $170 billion in projects are in the pipeline, underscoring Chile's ambition to become a global hydrogen leader.

Technology & Innovation Hydrogen technologies are advancing rapidly. Shell has secured renewable power purchase agreements to supply its 100-MW Refhyne II hydrogen electrolyser, ensuring a significant share of green electricity for operations. In the US, Samsung E&A will engineer hydrogen and power systems for a $8 billion sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) plant in Louisiana, targeting annual production of 200 million gallons of hydrogen-based jet fuel.

NASA has also awarded contracts for liquid hydrogen supply, with Plug Power set to deliver up to 480,000 pounds, supporting both aerospace and broader hydrogen market development.

Smart grid innovation is progressing in Central Europe. The European Climate, Infrastructure and Environment Executive Agency (CINEA) has reviewed three CEF-supported smart grid projects across Germany, Czechia, and Slovakia. These initiatives aim to enhance grid flexibility and integration of renewables, supporting regional energy security and decarbonization goals (see CINEA).

Funding & Projects Significant investments are flowing into hydrogen and grid modernization. The SAF plant in Louisiana represents a major commitment to low-carbon fuels for aviation. In Chile, the green hydrogen sector has attracted record levels of investment, with $40 billion in projects under environmental review. European Union funding continues to support smart grid upgrades, facilitating cross-border power flows and digitalization.

Risks & Constraints Despite momentum in clean energy, several constraints persist. The IEA warns that without stronger climate policies, oil and gas demand could grow until 2050. Structural barriers, such as regulatory uncertainty and infrastructure gaps, may limit the effective deployment of hydrogen and smart grid solutions. In the EV sector, S&P Global reports that expansion of charging infrastructure is slowing as demand moderates, except in China and South Asia where growth remains strong.

Outlook The energy transition is progressing unevenly. Hydrogen and smart grid investments are accelerating, but fossil fuel demand remains resilient. Policy clarity, regulatory reforms, and targeted funding will be critical to overcoming barriers and achieving decarbonization targets. Regional differences in technology adoption and infrastructure buildout will shape the pace and direction of change in the coming years.
